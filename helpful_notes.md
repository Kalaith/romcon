## The Fantasy

Everyone loves a good romance. Its modern audience being mostly female is, in my opinion, mostly cultural. That’s sort of sweeping and subjective, I know—the rest of this section will be, too. There are plenty of exceptions; forgive my lack of tact.

LitRPGs and romances have a big core similarity: they have elements of wish fulfillment in them, to propel the reader along.

### The litRPG fantasy is:

- You have great potential. Through diligence and dedication and hard work, you can grow more powerful than you or your doubters ever considered.
- The world has rules, and if you’re clever, you can master them.
- The underpinnings of reality can be understood, quantified, and harnessed to catapult you from humble beginnings to a place of mastery over your environment.

### The romance fantasy is:

- You are worthy of love. You don’t have to pretend to be someone else or hide who you are in order to find love.
- The right person will see you completely and love you completely.
- Love is powerful and will triumph. Seemingly insurmountable differences or obstacles will be conquered by it.
- The enemies will become lovers; the tsundere’s heart will melt; the hero will rescue the princess.

Another more cynical way of phrasing these two fantasy statements:

- You do not have to change to be worthy of the hottie’s love.
- The hottie will change for you.

That’s why so many romances have billionaire gangster CEO werewolf bad boys as the love interest falling for a petite brunette librarian or whatever.

That doesn’t need to be your thing. Love has to win, but it doesn’t have to be an intrinsic challenge of personality. It can also be an extrinsic one, where an ideal relationship is threatened by the world outside.

Many trad romances marketed towards women use intrinsic challenges to pressure the MCs. The monster to be fought is doubt, insecurity, possessiveness, etc.

Many romances in games, anime, and other stuff marketed toward guys use extrinsic challenges. The monster to be fought is a big, scary monster that wants to eat your beloved.

As an RR writer, I suggest you use more of these. Royal Road readers dislike unreasonable or immature actions from their protagonists or love interests.

## The Formula

If you’re reading this guide, it’s possible you haven’t done this before, or you tried and didn’t like the way it went. I am going to hold your hands in my hands when I tell you this so you will know I care about you, and you will be brave and receive this in good faith:

I want you to give the Formula a try.

I know that “formulaic” is shorthand for bad. You want to be original and unexpected and subversive. I promise there will be room for that between the beats.

Every genre has formulae. Quite often, you are writing in them already, without realizing it, because you are so familiar with your genre of choice that it has turned invisible for you. Romance’s formula has been tried and tested many times. Just as we know what makes a good litRPG tick, we have tons of evidence about what makes a romance story work.

Try this formula. Hit these beats. I’ve tried to keep them as open-ended as I can but you might see this and think it’s too prescriptive. There are a ton of stories you can tell with The Formula, and you can surprise and delight with no need to subvert it.

This is like the light outlines an artist makes of a head at the beginning of a sketch. It’s here to frame your self-expression into a form your reader can appreciate without friction. Nobody’s gonna notice the lines; they’ll be too busy staring into your beautiful eyes.

When you have used the formula successfully once, you may level up to Tier 2 and start skipping bits or remixing them or twisting them around. To continue our metaphor: once you’ve figured out where the eyes are supposed to go, THEN you can do a Picasso.

I’ve included percentages here of roughly how far along in the plot’s word count these beats usually come. It’s just for reference, especially if this is a subplot and not the main; don’t feel bound by them.

If you aren’t trying to write a primary romance, you can treat the formula like a subplot overlaid onto your main story, mapping its beats to the ones that make sense in the narrative of your MC and their big adventure. You’ll find that the romance formula is actually pretty similar to that of a fantasy adventure, in ways that should line up nicely.

## Part 1: Setting it up

### The Meet-Cute (1%)

The main characters meet. Let’s call them the main character (MC) and the love interest (LI).

- Your readers will always remember how it happened.
- They’ll go back and reread the first meeting multiple times.
- Make it compelling.
- The attraction might be instant, but just as often it’s not.
- Enemies-to-lovers is popular for a reason.

Plant the hint here about why these two characters are perfect for each other, but don’t overdo it. The situation isn’t right, for whatever reason.

### The First Refusal (5%)

The MC concludes that the relationship can’t happen. The key here is to make that conclusion a reasonable one.

- He’s a pauper and she’s a princess.
- He’s a knight and she’s a dragon.
- He’s a cop and she’s a robber.
- They’re both in an alien prison where if you kiss, your explosive collar blows up.

Make the gap real and imposing.

### The Problem Sticks Them Together (12%)

Now that you have a good reason the romance will not occur, glue the characters together.

Create a super-important or insurmountable reason, tied to the main plot, that they MUST be in close proximity to one another.

Maybe this is a quest they need one another to complete. Maybe it’s a horrible situation they’re both trapped in. Maybe it’s a competition they are opponents in.

Coworkers, roommates, sports rivals, arranged newlyweds, adventuring party companions. Due to circumstances beyond the MC’s control, they are now stuck in each other’s lives. If this romance is a subplot, then tie it into the main narrative.

## Part 2: Turning up the heat

### The Slow Burn (33%)

The problem develops. As it does, the MC and the LI slowly come to understand that, despite the obstacles, there is a mutual interest.

- Attraction, maybe, but if you want to really slow-cook it, friendship or respect is fine, too.
- Showcase the full scale of the gap keeping them apart.
- Show what they’ll need to reconcile before they can actually make this work.

As they spend time together, their true natures are gradually revealed. Their preconceptions about one another are challenged. Their original dynamic shifts. Counterarguments stack up against the first refusal reason.

### What if this could work? (40%)

An event occurs that forces the MC to finally come face-to-face with their attraction.

- The problem might cause this.
- In some stories this is the first intimate moment.
- In others the LI might save the MC’s life, or do something so lovable that it’s impossible to keep up pretenses.

They can’t pretend anymore.

### The Declaration (50%)

Another development finally breaks the wall down. One character confesses to the other.

The feelings are now out there for both to behold.

How do they react? This often manifests as a false high or a false low.

- False high: feelings are earnestly reciprocated and everything seems amazing. The characters open up to one another like never before. First kiss? Do they sleep with each other? Do they confide some great terrible secret?
- False low: the feelings are dashed. One character turns the other down, or they mutually agree to the love’s impossibility.

> BE CAREFUL when doing this. It’s a hard thing to read, especially for RR readers, who might eagerly give up on the romance right along with the protagonists.

### The New Baseline (66%)

The characters begin the next phase of their relationship, with clarity about how they now operate, or so they think.

But this isn’t the finish line. The reader should feel the shaking of instability in this form of their relationship.

There’s a crack in it, and over the course of this bit it grows. Their love—or their loneliness—is threatened by something intrinsic, extrinsic, or both.

- Intrinsic threat: a fear or false belief that hasn’t been addressed, an emotional block standing in the way of true happiness.
- Extrinsic threat: some function of their society prevents them from truly being together, or some curse, or some asshole with an army of skeletons.

Either way, it is a growing fissure in their new normal, and it develops further, building pressure up, until…

### The Blow-Up (75%)

The threat strikes. The worst thing that could happen does.

- Intrinsic blow-up: the fear or false belief might take over. They seek to regress to the baseline beliefs they had during the first refusal.
- Extrinsic blow-up: the climactic bad thing directly jeopardizes their love. An invasion, a kidnapping, a curse, an injury, or a disaster.

You can’t have a happily ever after if the evil sorcerer blows the world up.

> THIS PART CAN SCREW YOUR STORY.
>
> One of the biggest pet peeves readers have is a poorly written third act breakup (or 3AB), when characters lose the development and progress they earned for forced drama.
>
> This is the beat many people are thinking of when they say they don’t like romance. When you hear people talk about how characters in romance act like teenagers, they often mean the stories that mishandle this beat.

Think especially carefully before you cause the blow-up via a misunderstanding or miscommunication.

If it could be prevented by sitting down and talking it out like adults, make sure you have an excellent, reinforced, and telegraphed reason that cannot happen.

If your reader can fault the MC, they will, and on RR that causes 0.5s.

For my money, I think a romance subplot in an adventure is best served by an extrinsic blow-up. This lets you tie the romance in directly with the main plot.

That’s not to say intrinsic can’t work well, but it is much easier for it to run into the pitfalls above or be seen as a frustrating tangent instead of one of the pieces elevating your main narrative.

## Part 3: The Test and the Triumph

### It’s So Over (88%)

The crisis is here in full force. The love we’re all rooting for is now at the make-or-break point. The bond between the two main characters is tested for all it’s worth.

It looks like it might break… can love win?

### We’re So Back (90%)

The two leads pass the test. The threat they faced is overcome by the love they share.

- An “I OBJECT!” yelled at the arranged wedding to the antagonist.
- An anguished public declaration of love against all odds that banishes fear and doubt.
- A pitched 2-on-1 battle where the characters display how amazingly in-sync they are as they destroy the villain.

Their love is a key element to their victory.

### The Deal is Sealed (98%)

The last obstacle’s been knocked down. Give the people what they want.

- The big kiss
- The royal wedding
- The steamy love scene

Whatever catharsis you’ve been building up to, grant it. Pull no punches. Cringe no cringes.

If you’ve done your job right, the readers will be barking for this like a pack of hounds and you can go as overtly heart-on-your-sleeve gushy as you like—they’ll love it.

### Happily Ever After (100%)

In a Romance Main novel, this might be the epilogue.

We get a look at what the characters’ lives with each other now look like. They’re joyful, they’re happy, they’re safe. Maybe they’ve got a baby on the way.

This concludes the usual romance beat sheet. However, you’re writing on RR.

So unless you’ve managed to stretch this template across however many volumes you were planning to write, you now have two lovers who will have continuing adventures.

## The Continuing Adventures

Use whatever genre structure you please, and keep your narrative going, now with a happily coupled-up MC and LI at its center.

Here are some dos and don’ts for this long tail:

### DON’T pivot to harem

Not unless you made it clear from the outset that this was gonna be a harem. People do NOT LIKE surprise polyamory.

If your readers are informed and bought in, then go nuts. I don’t really know how to do this, though, so you’ll need to find a different guide.

### DON’T make the LI background noise

From now on, you’ve got a second-most-important character in the story (the LI). This is what you signed up for, and it’s what the people want.

Now that they’ve gotten together, do NOT relegate them to a background character or bit player. When you imagine the cover of your volume, stick them on there, too. Use them as a way to contextualize and explore your MC and your world.

### DON’T milk the intrinsic relationship for drama

Or if you do, be very cautious and sparing about it. Part of the romantic appeal is the Happily Ever After.

This isn’t to say it must be perfect; but if you decide to introduce strife or drama into their relationship, show us how their hard-won maturity and closeness allows them to figure things out and solve that problem before it does any lasting damage, ideally in the same chapter or close to it.

### DO continue to show its strength with extrinsic challenges

Your protagonists are a team now. They can trust and rely on each other.

- Make them bring out the best in each other.
- Allow them to flex the strength of that bond now and then.
- Show the ways they are in each other’s corner.

Your characters now have something to fight for, and a powerful weapon in one another’s trust.

When you threaten the relationship directly and explicitly—kidnappings or forced separations, etc—save it for really serious moments.

Do it too often and you’ll turn the relationship into a vulnerability, not a strength. It can be fun as a berserk button for the MC or LI in a “touch them and die” kind of way.

### DO continue to develop both characters

A love interest who stagnates when that love is consummated will remind people negatively of video game romances, when you run out of dialogue options and your amazing waifu becomes a mannequin tagging along and making kissy faces.

Give them progression. Give them interests and friends and objectives outside the relationship. You don’t need to attach them at the hip, BUT…

### DO keep the torch burning

Take opportunities to show the way the MCs maintain their romance.

- Cute gifts
- Cute dates
- Little physical displays of affection

There is a risk of overdoing it here—don’t make the MC entirely distracted or sand over the LI into a doe-eyed love doll. Keep their characterization in mind when thinking about the kind of romantic partner they might be.

It’s a great way to add dimension to a character, bringing out aspects you might not expect or reinforcing ones that appeal to you.

### DO put more relationship milestones into your story

The proposal, the wedding, the moving in together, the kids, the house, the discovering of elemental synergies that improve their attack power when close to one another.

You know, the usual stuff. You don’t need to spend as much narrative time on these as you did the initial romance, but it’s fun to do and checkpoints like this will keep the relationship interesting and vital.

An opportunity you have as a serialized author is that readers are hungry to see all the things that come after Happily Ever After, which most romances elide.
